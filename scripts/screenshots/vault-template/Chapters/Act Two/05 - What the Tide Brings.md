"Kestrel Point, do you copy, over."

"This is Kestrel Point," Mara said into the radio, and was surprised by how steady her own voice sounded. "Go ahead, *Marguerite*."

Her father was already moving — badly, favoring the leg, but moving with a speed that startled her — pulling charts across the workbench, marking the north shoal with a stub of pencil worn down to almost nothing. "Ask them how many aboard. Ask them if they've got a fix on their position."

She asked. The answer came back broken by static: three aboard, taking water fast, no clear position in the dark and the driving rain, engine failing.

"They can't see the light," her father said, half to himself, half to her, already working the problem the way she imagined he'd worked a hundred problems like it over thirty years. "That's the whole trouble. They're navigating blind in a sea they don't know, and our beam's the only fixed point they've got, and this storm's throwing enough spray to cut visibility to nothing." He looked up at her, and for the first time since she'd arrived, he looked less like her father and more like a keeper — someone whose whole life had built toward exactly this kind of night. "The fog signal. Get to the fog signal and sound it, long and steady. Sound carries further than light in this. Give them something to steer by."

"You can't climb down to the signal house, not on that leg—"

"I'm not asking you to fetch me. I'm telling you to go."

She went.

The fog signal house stood forty yards from the tower, along a path she remembered as a child and could not now see at all, the rain coming so hard it felt less like weather and more like an assault, something with intent behind it. She found the door by feel, found the mechanism by the memory in her hands rather than her eyes, and threw the switch her father had shown her once, twenty years ago, as a game — *this is what you do if you're ever the only one here and something goes wrong* — never imagining she would be standing here in the dark, soaked through, actually doing it.

The horn sounded, a long low note that seemed to come up through the ground itself, out into the storm, over and over, a voice for the light when the light alone wasn't enough.

She stood there sounding it by hand, over and over, until her arms ached and her ears rang, straining into the dark for any sign of the *Marguerite* — a running light, an engine, a shout — anything at all to tell her the sound was landing somewhere, that somewhere out in all that black water, three people were finding their way home by it.
