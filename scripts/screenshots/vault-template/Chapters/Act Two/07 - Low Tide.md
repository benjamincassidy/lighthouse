Morning came in pale and scoured-looking, the storm having burned itself out sometime before dawn, leaving behind the particular stillness that follows any large violence — the sea flat and grey and unbothered, as though it hadn't spent the night trying to take three lives and had already forgotten the attempt.

Mara found her father in the watch room, log open in front of him, pen in hand for the first time in days.

*March 22,* he was writing, slow and deliberate, the letters shakier than they used to be but still, unmistakably, his own hand. *Storm force 10, worst of the season. Marguerite ran aground safe, three souls recovered, no injuries beyond cuts and cold. Fog signal sounded by hand for one hour twenty minutes. Light maintained throughout, fourteen-second interval, no deviation.*

He paused, pen hovering, and for a moment Mara thought that was the whole entry. Then he added one more line, and did not look up while he wrote it, which was, she was learning, exactly how her father said the things that cost him something to say.

*Signal sounded by my daughter, Mara Voss, who I did not train for this and who did it better than most keepers I've known in thirty years on this rock.*

She read it over his shoulder and had to look away toward the window for a while, out at the flat grey morning sea, before she trusted her voice again.

"The trust board," she said finally. "The automation. Are you still going to fight them on it?"

Her father set the pen down and was quiet long enough that she thought he wasn't going to answer at all.

"A solar unit doesn't climb down and sound a horn by hand in a force ten gale," he said eventually. "A solar unit doesn't know the difference between a real emergency and a sensor fault at three in the morning. Doesn't know these waters, doesn't know that channel's changed forty feet in the last decade the same as I know it because I've watched it happen, inch by inch, year by year, for thirty years." He looked at her then, really looked, in a way she didn't think he had since she'd stepped off the ferry. "But I'm seventy-one years old and I broke my leg falling down my own stairs, and I can't keep pretending that isn't going to happen again. Maybe worse, next time. Maybe with no one here to answer the radio."

"So what happens to the light?"

"That," he said, "depends rather a lot on what you're planning to do with the rest of your life."

Mara looked out at the water — flat, patient, indifferent, the same sea that had nearly killed three strangers twelve hours ago and this morning looked as if it had never held a storm at all — and found, for the first time in twelve years, that she did not immediately know how to answer him.
