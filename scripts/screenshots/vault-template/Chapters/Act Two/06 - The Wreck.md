They came out of the dark almost on top of the rocks — a running light, low and wrong, listing hard to port, close enough that for one terrible second Mara was certain she was about to watch a boat break apart in front of her.

"Here!" she shouted, uselessly, the wind tearing the word away before it had traveled ten feet. She kept sounding the horn instead, one long blast after another, and adjusted the angle of the beam with the hand signal her father had taught her from the tower window above — left, further left, that's it, hold there — until the *Marguerite* found the narrow deep-water channel between the shoal and the point, the one channel that hadn't drowned three ships in living memory, the one channel the light and the horn together had just shown them through the blind dark.

The boat ran aground, hard but controlled, on the shingle below the tower rather than the rocks beyond it. Close enough to that difference to matter more than anything Mara had ever done in her life.

She half-climbed, half-fell down the path to the shingle, where Ronnie — soaked, bleeding from a cut above one eye, laughing with the particular hysteria of a man who has just watched himself not die — was hauling a woman and a boy up out of the surf, the *Marguerite*'s single deckhand close behind them, all three moving with the loose-jointed clumsiness of people whose bodies haven't yet caught up to the fact that they're safe.

"She heard the horn," the woman was saying, over and over, to no one in particular, to everyone. "We couldn't see a thing, the light was there but we couldn't tell how far, and then the horn — we followed the horn."

Mara helped drag them clear of the tideline, wrapped the boy in her own coat, and did not trust herself to speak until they were all inside the base of the tower, dripping onto stone floors that had seen a hundred years of exactly this kind of arrival.

Her father was waiting at the bottom of the stairs, having somehow gotten himself down them despite everything she'd told him not to, leaning hard on the rail, his face grey with pain and something that might have been pride, might have been simple relief, might have been both at once.

"You sounded it steady," he said. "Perfect rhythm. Didn't waver once."

"I was terrified the whole time."

"That's not the same thing as wavering." He said it like it was the most obvious fact in the world, a keeper's fact, learned the hard way over thirty years alone on a rock in the sea. "Terrified and steady is the only kind of brave that's ever actually useful, Mara. It's the only kind I ever managed myself."

Outside, through the tower's small round window, the light kept turning — fourteen seconds, exactly as it always had — sweeping its long white blade out over water that had, tonight, given three people back instead of taking them.
