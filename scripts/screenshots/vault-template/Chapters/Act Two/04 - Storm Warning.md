The second storm came in faster than the shipping forecast had promised, and by the time Mara understood what was happening, the island was already cut off.

"That's not unusual," her father said, when she told him, with more calm than she thought the situation warranted. He was sitting up now, leg braced, directing her through the lamp checks from his chair with the patient irritation of a man watching someone else do a job he'd rather be doing himself. "Ronnie won't come out in this. Nobody with sense would. We ride it out. We always have."

"We meaning you. Alone."

"We meaning whoever's here." He said it without any particular weight, but she felt it land anyway. "You cleaned the lens wrong yesterday, by the way. Too much pressure on the west face, you'll scratch the coating. Lighter touch."

"I did it exactly like you taught me when I was nine."

"You were a careful child. You've gotten heavy-handed in your old age."

It was, she realized, the closest thing to a joke he had made since she arrived, and something in her chest loosened slightly at the sound of it, an old door swinging on a hinge she'd thought had rusted shut.

By nightfall the wind had climbed past anything the log recorded that season, and the tower itself seemed to breathe with it, a low structural groan on every gust that Mara felt more than heard, through the soles of her feet. Rain came sideways, hard enough to sting through a coat. She climbed the spiral stairs with her father's voice trailing up after her — *left rail, mind the eleventh step, it's always been loose* — and stood in the lamp room watching the great lens turn, slow and enormous and unbothered, fourteen seconds exactly, while the whole sky came apart beyond the glass.

She understood, standing there, something she had spent twelve years refusing to understand: that this had never been about choosing the light over her. It had been about believing, with the particular stubbornness of a man who had lost his wife and did not intend to lose anything else on his watch, that the work of keeping people safe did not stop simply because it was hard, or lonely, or unwitnessed. That someone had to stand in the storm so that other people didn't have to.

She thought of her father crawling down these same stairs three nights ago, leg broken, refusing to let the light go dark even as his own body failed him.

The radio crackled to sudden, startling life on the workbench behind her — Ronnie's voice, thin with static, cutting through the storm.

*"Kestrel Point, Kestrel Point, this is the* Marguerite. *We're taking on water off the north shoal. Repeat, we are taking on water. Do you copy?"*

Mara's hand was already moving toward the radio before she'd finished deciding to answer it.
