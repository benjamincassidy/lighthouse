Kestrel Point looked smaller than she remembered, and also larger, in the way that childhood places always do — smaller in scale, larger in weight.

The harbor hadn't changed. Same grey stone jetty, same row of houses facing the water with their backs turned stubbornly against the wind, same smell of tar and kelp and diesel that she would have known blindfolded anywhere in the world. What had changed was the quiet. She remembered the town as loud — gulls, engines, her father's voice carrying across the yard when he called her in for supper. Now the streets were empty in the flat grey light of afternoon, and the few people she passed looked at her the way you look at weather moving in: carefully, and from a distance.

Mrs. Pell at the general store recognized her before she'd said a word.

"You've his eyes," the old woman said, by way of greeting, as though no time had passed at all. "Come for your father, have you."

"Is he—" Mara stopped. She didn't know how to finish the sentence in a way that didn't sound like she was already grieving.

"He's alive, if that's what you're asking. Ronnie took the boat out two days back to check on him. Found him at the base of the tower, hurt his leg coming down the stairs too fast in the dark, but alive. Radio's been out since the storm before last — that's why nobody's heard from him. He wouldn't leave the light unattended to walk to a telephone, not even for that. You know your father."

She did know her father. That was precisely the problem.

"He's refusing to come off the rock," Mrs. Pell went on, ringing up a tin of tea Mara hadn't meant to buy. "Trust board wants to automate the light — bring in one of those solar units, no keeper needed at all. Man's spent thirty years of his life on that island and they want to replace him with a computer chip in a plastic box. He says he'll die out there before he lets them switch off a light he's tended by hand."

"That's not a metaphor, is it."

"No," Mrs. Pell said. "I don't believe it is."

Outside, the wind had shifted, and for a moment — just a moment — Mara thought she saw it: a pale smear against the darkening horizon, out past the last of the rocks, gone before she could be certain it was real. She stood on the jetty a long time afterward, watching the place where the light should have been, doing the thing she had promised herself on the ferry she would not do.

She started counting again.
