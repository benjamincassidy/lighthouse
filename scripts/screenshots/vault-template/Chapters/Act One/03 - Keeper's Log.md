Her father kept a log, as all keepers did, and Mara found the current volume open on the table in the watch room, the pen still resting in the crease of the page as though he had set it down mid-thought and simply never come back to it.

*March 14. Wind NE, force 6, building. Lamp serviced, lens cleaned. Fog signal tested — working.*

*March 15. Wind NE, force 9. Radio mast down in the night, will need Ronnie to bring the part. Lens cleaned. Everything else as it should be.*

*March 16. Storm continuing. Leg troubling me on the stairs — favor the left rail going down. Lamp serviced. Fourteen seconds, same as always. She would not know it if she saw it now, but I have kept it exactly as her mother taught me, not a half-second early or late in thirty years. Some things a man can still be sure of.*

There the entries stopped, three days of blank pages after, and then, in a hand less steady than the rest:

*March 19. Fell on the stairs. Leg is bad but the light is not the leg's problem, it is mine, and I intend to keep it. Ronnie came. Told him I was fine. He did not believe me and he was right not to. If Mara reads this — and she will, because she was always the one who read the log when she thought I wasn't watching, since she was seven years old — I want her to know the light did not go dark. Not once. Whatever else happens to this old leg, the light did not go dark.*

Mara sat down hard in the keeper's chair, the log open on her knees, and found she could not immediately stand back up.

She had spent twelve years telling people her father was a difficult man, stubborn to the point of foolishness, who had chosen a rock and a lamp over every other kind of life a person could have — over her mother, in the end, over Mara herself when she'd needed him to choose otherwise. She had built a whole version of him out of that stubbornness, flat and simple, easy to be angry at from a safe distance.

The log did not match the version she had built. The log was the record of a man who had crawled, if that was what it took, to make sure a light kept turning for ships he would never see and sailors he would never meet, because thirty years ago someone had taught him that the work mattered more than whether anyone noticed it was being done.

She found him an hour later, asleep in the small bedroom off the watch room, his bad leg propped on a folded blanket, breathing evenly. She did not wake him. She went back up into the lamp room instead, found the maintenance kit exactly where the log said it would be, and began — clumsily, uncertainly, her hands remembering a little more than her mind expected them to — the work of cleaning the lens.
