The trust board sent a man in a grey suit on the first ferry of the following week, carrying a folder of glossy brochures for a solar-powered beacon that required, the brochures said, no keeper at all.

Mara met him at the jetty before he'd finished stepping off the boat.

"Mr. Ainsley. I understand you're here about the light."

He had the practiced smile of a man who delivered unwelcome news for a living and had long since stopped letting it bother him. "Ms. Voss. I was told your father would be handling this conversation."

"My father is up in the lamp room, where he belongs, and I'm handling it instead. I've filed the paperwork this morning — I'm applying for the assistant keeper position, effective immediately, with a review toward full keeper status once the board's satisfied I know what I'm doing." She said it plainly, the way her father said things when he had already made up his mind and was simply informing the world of the fact rather than asking its permission. "I grew up on this light. I know its history, I know these waters, and as of last week I have a written account from three rescued sailors and the harbor authority describing exactly what a keeper on this rock is capable of doing that your solar unit is not. I'd like the board to read it before anyone signs anything."

Ainsley studied her for a long moment, the professional smile faltering into something more genuinely uncertain. "That's rather a significant career change."

"I edit textbooks for a publishing house in the city. It pays well and I am, by any reasonable measure, extremely good at being bored by it." She looked past him, up at the tower, where she could just make out her father's shape in the lamp room window, watching this exchange with the particular stillness of a man trying very hard not to interfere. "I spent twelve years telling myself I left this place because my father chose the light over everything else. It took a storm and a fog horn and a boy I pulled out of the surf with my own hands to understand that he didn't choose the light instead of us. He chose it because it was how he took care of us. All of us. Every boat that ever passed this point in the dark."

"The board will need more than sentiment, Ms. Voss."

"I know. That's why I brought paperwork, not sentiment." She held out the folder. "Read it on the crossing back. You'll have my formal application by Friday."

That evening, she climbed the spiral stairs one final time before the ferry, past the eleventh step that still, after everything, remained loose, and found her father in the lamp room exactly where he always was at this hour, watching the lens complete its slow, unhurried rotation against the coming dark.

"Assistant keeper," he said, without turning around. "That's a demotion for you, isn't it. Give up the city for a rock in the sea and a title with 'assistant' in front of it."

"You held that title for two years before they made you keeper outright. I expect I'll manage."

He turned then, and there was something in his face she hadn't seen there since she was small — not pride exactly, or not only pride, but something closer to recognition, as if he were finally looking at a person he'd always hoped she might become and had, somewhere along the way, stopped quite believing he'd get to see.

Outside, the light began its long white sweep across the darkening water, fourteen seconds exactly, steady as it had been for thirty years, steady as it would be, Mara understood now, for a good while yet.

She started counting, the way she always had. This time, for the first time in twelve years, she did not stop before she reached the end.
